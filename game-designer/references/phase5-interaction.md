# Phase 5 — Interaction & Mechanics

**Goal:** Finalize the specific mechanics — what players can and cannot do — and ensure
the interaction between players/rules creates the depth promised in earlier phases.

---

## Core Concepts to Apply

**Mechanics** = the actions, behaviors, and mechanisms a designer uses to produce gameplay.
What players call "rules," designers call mechanics.

**Depth** = the ability for a player's decisions to result in meaningfully different outcomes.
More depth = more replayability, longer mastery curve, sustained engagement.

**Breadth** = the number of choices available at a given moment. 2–5 is ideal.
More than ~7 risks analysis paralysis.

**Elegance** = high depth, low complexity. Always the goal.
Worst case: high complexity, low depth (hard to learn, boring to play).

**Interaction types:**
- Direct: "I attack you"
- Indirect: "I block the resource you need"
- Environmental: "I trigger an event that affects everyone"
- Asymmetric: different players have different abilities or goals

---

## Questions to Work Through

### 5a. Player Actions
> "On a player's turn, what can they actually *do*? List every possible action — even rough
> ones. We'll trim and sharpen from there."

After they list, evaluate each action:
- Does it create meaningful decisions?
- Does it interact with other players (directly or indirectly)?
- Does it serve the goal/opposition structure we built?

Flag any actions that are "always correct" (no decision) or "never useful" (dead weight).

### 5b. Interaction Design
> "How do players affect each other? Is it direct (attack, steal, block) or indirect
> (compete for resources, race to locations)? Or some of both?"

Ask: "Could a player ignore everyone else and still win? If so, does that feel right for
your game type?" (For a cooperative game, yes. For a competitive game, probably not.)

### 5c. Asymmetry (Optional)
> "Do all players play the same way, or do different players have different abilities,
> roles, or goals?"

Asymmetry adds depth but also complexity. Help them decide if it fits their design goals
and target player.

### 5d. Mechanics Evaluation
Run each core mechanic through the design evaluation axes from Module 2:

| Axis | Question |
|------|----------|
| **Innovation** | Is this mechanic unique or surprising? |
| **Depth** | Does it create meaningful decisions? |
| **Complexity** | How many sentences to explain it? |
| **Cohesion** | Does it fit the theme and game feeling? |
| **Satisfaction** | Is it fun to execute? Does it feel good? |

Flag anything that scores low on Depth or Cohesion.

### 5e. Core Game Loop (Final)
> "Let's state your core game loop in 5 steps or fewer. Start of turn to end of turn,
> one cycle."

This will feed directly into the final rules sheet.

### 5f. Prototype Reality Check
> "If you had to make a paper prototype of this game right now — just index cards,
> tokens, and a grid drawn on paper — what would you need? Does anything feel impossible
> to prototype simply?"

If something is hard to prototype, it's often too complex. Encourage simplification.

