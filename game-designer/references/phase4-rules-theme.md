# Phase 4 — Rules, Theme & Narrative

**Goal:** Give the game a cohesive thematic wrapper that makes mechanics intuitive and
immersive, and begin crystallizing the formal rules.

---

## Core Concepts to Apply

**Thematic** = the unifying idea present throughout mechanics, narrative, art direction,
and audio. It's the "skin" over the mechanical skeleton.

**Resonance** = when theme and mechanics reinforce each other, making both feel inevitable.
Example: A game about fire spreading is resonant if the board mechanic involves zones
becoming progressively blocked — fire *does* spread, so no explanation needed.

**Dissonance** = when theme and mechanics clash, pulling players out of immersion.
Example: A horror-themed game with cheerful music.

**Piggybacking** = using known real-world behavior to explain a mechanic without extra
complexity. Example: everyone knows fire spreads, so you can skip explaining a
"spreading hazard" mechanic if it's called fire.

**Complexity** = how much information a player needs to hold to make a decision. It is a
*cost*, not a feature. Always prefer lower complexity for the same gameplay depth.

**Elegance** = high depth, low complexity. The ideal ratio. Chess is elegant: simple
rules, infinite depth.

**Narrative** = the "why" that explains the conflict. Even abstract games benefit from
at least a sentence: "You are rival merchants competing for harbor rights." It makes
the opposition feel motivated rather than arbitrary.

---

## Questions to Work Through

### 4a. Theme Brainstorm
> "Let's think about the world your game lives in. What's the setting or theme? Don't
> filter yourself — give me 3 to 5 wild ideas."

If stuck, seed with restrictions to spark creativity:
- "What if it was set in [a specific historical period / a genre / a mundane location]?"
- "What if the players weren't human?"
- "What's a theme you've never seen in a game but would love to?"

### 4b. Theme Evaluation
After they brainstorm, help them evaluate each idea against:
1. Does it naturally explain the conflict (opposition feels motivated)?
2. Can the mechanics be named/framed to piggyback on what we know about this world?
3. Does it match the game feeling from Phase 1?
4. Is there a thematic fit for the player characters and the "enemy" or obstacle?

### 4c. Theme Selection + Narrative
> "Which theme feels most exciting to you? Let's pick one and write your game's
> elevator pitch: 'You play as [players] who want to [goal], but [opposition].'"

Help them fill in the template until it's crisp and specific.

### 4d. Ability/Mechanic Naming
> "Now let's rename your core mechanics to fit the theme. What's the thematic name for
> [core action 1]? For [core action 2]?"

Example: In a pirate game, "Move 2 spaces" becomes "Sail to the next island."
"Take 1 damage" becomes "Take on water."

This is where piggybacking pays off — thematic names reduce rules-reading.

### 4e. Complexity Audit
> "Let's count how many rules a new player needs to remember before their first turn.
> Can we get it under five core rules?"

Flag anything that feels complex. Ask: "Is there a simpler way to achieve the same
gameplay effect?"
